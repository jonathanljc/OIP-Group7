Day 1
Research & Discovery

Project Kickoff & Initial Research

Today's Focus: Engaging with our stakeholder (Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens) to align on project goals, constraints, and expectations.

  • Met with the stakeholder to understand operational needs and vision
  • Clarified constraints and success criteria
  • Confirmed project scope and target outcomes
Reflection

The stakeholder meeting with Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens was instrumental in grounding our project in real-world operational needs. Beyond confirming the scope, it clarified the heritage-related limitations we must respect, which will significantly shape our solution design. We also gained early insights into their priorities and constraints. This helped us shift from abstract problem-solving to a user-centered, constraint-aware approach. Having this alignment early ensures that our research, ideation, and prototyping phases will remain focused, purposeful, and contextually relevant.

Day 2
Research & Discovery

User Interviews & Requirements Gathering

Today's Focus: Conducting user interviews and gathering functional requirements.

  • Completed 5 user interviews with diverse visitor groups
  • Documented key user needs
Reflection

Interviews revealed unexpected openness to digital tools across age groups, challenging assumptions about tech adoption among older visitors. At the same time, differing comfort levels with technology highlight the need for inclusive, intuitive design. Balancing this with heritage preservation constraints adds another layer of complexity, requiring thoughtful integration of modern solutions within a protected, historical environment. These insights sharpened our focus on designing with empathy, flexibility, and contextual awareness.

Day 3
Ideation & Selection

Divergent Thinking & Solution Brainstorming

Today's Focus: Exploring solution possibilities

  • Reframed challenges using “How Might We” statement
  • Generated 6 concept ideas addressing inclusivity, heritage compliance, and cost
Reflection

The “How Might We” method helped the team approach the problem from multiple angles. It encouraged inclusive thinking while giving space for bold ideas. Some team members leaned toward tech-heavy solutions, while others advocated for simplicity and accessibility, leading to rich discussions. These differing viewpoints helped us consider not just what was possible, but what was practical and appropriate for all visitor types.

Day 4
Ideation & Selection

Convergent Analysis & Prioritization

Today's Focus: Narrowing down concepts using a prioritization matrix.

  • Assessed each idea based on user value and technical complexity
  • Mapped solutions on a prioritization matrix
  • Identified a QR code system as the most feasible
Reflection

The prioritization matrix helped us move from open-ended ideation to strategic decision-making. By visualizing each solution’s user value against technical complexity, we could clearly see which ideas were feasible and impactful. Some team members initially favored more novel or high-tech solutions, but the matrix grounded our thinking in what could realistically be delivered within constraints. This tool bridged creative ambition with practical execution, aligning the team around a well-balanced direction forward.

Day 5
Ideation & Selection

Stakeholder Validation & Refinement

Today's Focus: Presenting and validating shortlisted idea with stakeholder.

  • Shared QR-based idea with Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens
  • Received critical feedback on smartphone limitations and inclusivity
  • Identified key touchpoints and potential friction areas
Reflection

Stakeholder input was pivotal in shaping our direction. Initially, we leaned toward a sleek QR-only system, thinking it would streamline the experience. However, feedback from the Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens revealed practical concerns, such as children not having smartphones. This pushed us to reconsider our assumptions and prioritize inclusivity. Their perspective helped us bridge the gap between ideal design and real-world usage. It also underscored the value of early stakeholder engagement in preventing costly design missteps later.

Day 6
Ideation & Selection

Final Selection & Concept Refinement

Today's Focus: Finalizing the chosen solution and preparing for prototyping.

  • Refined our direction toward a dual-option (QR + Kiosk) model
Reflection

Our refinement process was driven by empathy and a deep respect for the diversity of the Kibble Palace’s audience. We moved beyond assumptions by re-evaluating our concept through multiple lenses: accessibility, equity, and device availability. The result was a dual-system that accommodates both tech-savvy and tech-averse users without compromising simplicity or elegance. This refinement highlighted the importance of inclusive design as an active, evolving process, not a one-time decision. It also reminded us that thoughtful integration of physical and digital touchpoints can elevate the visitor experience without alienating any demographic.

Day 7
Prototyping & Testing

Storyboarding & Low-Fidelity Prototypes

Today's Focus: Mapping the visitor journey and creating paper prototypes.

  • Created comprehensive storyboards to visualize the full visitor experience
  • Identified the key pain point: users often forget to check out
  • Introduced AR feature to enhance engagement and act as a subtle reminder for users to check out
  • Developed lo-fi prototypes
Reflection

The storyboarding process challenged us to think from the visitor’s perspective rather than just a system design lens. Mapping the journey visually made it easier to empathize with different users, especially those unfamiliar with digital check-ins. A major realization was how easily people could forget to check out, which had direct implications on data accuracy. This insight reframed our solution from being purely functional to one that also subtly nudges user behavior, which later shaped our decision to integrate AR as a contextual reminder. During lo-fi prototyping, the simplicity of paper sketches was liberating. We weren’t bogged down by aesthetics or technical limitations, which made room for faster ideation and open critique. Everyone on the team could contribute equally regardless of technical background, which enhanced collaboration and surfaced edge cases we hadn’t considered. We also learned that some design ideas that sounded strong in theory didn’t translate well when visualized, saving us time before entering hi-fi development.

Day 8
Prototyping & Testing

Hi-Fidelity Interactive Prototypes

Today's Focus: Building and refining high-fidelity prototypes in Figma

  • Created high-fidelity Figma prototypes
Reflection

Moving from lo-fi to hi-fi prototypes deepened our understanding of user interaction. Paper sketches helped us quickly explore structure, but only digital prototypes exposed real usability nuances, like visual hierarchy, interaction timing, and accessibility. It also gave stakeholders greater clarity and confidence, helping align expectations across the team. Most importantly, the process reminded us that good design isn't static, it evolves. What looked perfect on paper had to be challenged, rebuilt, and reshaped once real interactions came into play. And that evolution is what made the final prototype not just functional, but truly user-aligned.

Day 9
Prototyping & Testing

User Testing

Today's Focus: Conducting hands-on usability testing

  • Tested prototypes with 5 users
  • Collected detailed usability feedback
  • Identified usability issues and areas for refinement
Reflection

User testing was a pivotal turning point that exposed the gap between assumed and actual user behavior. Where we thought users would breeze through interfaces, we instead saw pauses, repeated actions, or tentative inputs. These micro-behaviors reflected uncertainty, underscoring that even minor interface decisions carry significant cognitive weight. The most profound takeaway was that inclusive design goes beyond accessibility. It requires anticipating not just who can use the system, but how they feel while using it. This insight shaped our final refinements, ensuring both tech-forward and tech-averse users could complete their journey without stress or confusion.

Day 10
Prototyping & Testing

Final Refinements and Project Completion

Today's Focus: Implementing improvements and preparing project completion deliverables

  • Changed “pax” to “person” for better clarity
  • Reduced checkout code from 6 digits to 3 for easier recall
  • Added confirmation prompt to avoid accidental checkouts
  • Prepare poster and website deliverables for submission
Reflection

The final stretch was driven by user feedback. Refining our design based on usability testing gave us confidence that our solution addressed real user needs, not just assumptions. As we shifted into final deliverable preparation, we faced the challenge of turning days of iterative exploration into a clear, compelling narrative. This wasn’t just about organizing visuals, it required team alignment on the “why” behind each decision. It was an opportunity to reconnect with our initial goals, validate our design logic, and communicate value with intention and clarity to external audiences.
