User Interviews and Insights

Primary Research

Key Interview Findings

Visitors are open to using digital tools in public spaces.
Most users report a positive experience when interacting with digital features.
Tech-savviness is not limited to youth. Older visitors also show confidence using digital interfaces.

Core User Needs & Requirements

5 Key Requirements

Seamless User Experience

Entry and exit should be quick, intuitive, and non-intrusive.

Accurate and Comprehensive Insights

The system should accurately track visitor counts, capture demographic breakdowns, and generate metrics useful for planning and funding applications.

Heritage Compliance

No structural changes or permanent fixtures; solution must respect conservation requirements of listed buildings.

Minimal Budget Availability

Implementation must be low-cost or free, avoiding reliance on expensive tech infrastructure or ongoing license fees.

Non-Disruptive & Discreet Deployment

Any solution must blend into the existing environment and not interrupt or alter the natural visitor experience.

Pre-existing Solution

Previous Attempt

Cellphone Signal Tracking

Previously attempted by stakeholder

The Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens previously attempted to implement a cellphone signal tracking system to monitor visitor numbers. This passive approach aimed to detect mobile devices pinging for Wi-Fi connections or cellular signals to estimate visitor counts.

Initial Appeal

  • Completely passive - no visitor interaction required
  • Automated data collection
  • Non-intrusive to the visitor experience
  • Potential for continuous monitoring

Why It Failed

  • Technical Complexity: Required sophisticated filtering and analytics to interpret signals
  • Accuracy Problems: Significant variance between actual and detected visitor numbers

Lessons Learned

We learned that passive solutions like cellphone tracking often lack accuracy. Without users actively choosing to engage, such systems can't reliably distinguish between individuals, groups, or repeat visitors, leading to vague and unusable data. Additionally, from our research, we found that overly complex technologies often fail due to maintenance issues, unclear instructions, or limited staff capacity. This reinforced the value of simple, and sustainable designs.
